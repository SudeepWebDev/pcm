Real Example: FM Radio Transmission

In FM radio broadcasting, a pilot tone at 19 kHz is transmitted alongside the main stereo signal to help receivers decode the left and right audio channels. The carrier frequency might be around 100 MHz for FM radio, while message signals (audio) range from 20 Hz to 15 kHz.

Example Parameters:

  • Carrier Frequency (fc) ≈ 1000 Hz (scaled down for demo)
  • Pilot Frequency (fp) ≈ 100 Hz
  • Message Frequency (fm) ≈ 10 Hz

This example shows how a pilot signal is essential for synchronization and signal demodulation in real-world applications like FM broadcasting.

Mathematical Model Verification

The modulated signal with pilot is given by:

s(t) = (1 + m(t)) × cos(2πfct) + 0.2 × cos(2πfpt)

where m(t) = sin(2πfmt)

Without the pilot, the modulated signal simplifies to:

s(t) = (1 + m(t)) × cos(2πfct)

✅ Our simulation plots follow these formulas correctly:

  • "With Pilot" includes an extra sinusoidal component at pilot frequency.
  • "Without Pilot" shows only the message-modulated carrier.

Technical Details: Pilot-Carrier Modulation

What Is Pilot-Carrier Modulation?

  • A small, attenuated carrier (the "pilot") is reinserted into an SSB-suppressed signal after filtering.
  • Pilot level typically 16–26 dB below the full carrier amplitude.
  • Provides a frequency reference for the receiver's coherent demodulator/AFC.

Why It's Needed

  • Frequency Stability: Pure SSB (J3E) suppresses the carrier entirely → receiver has no reference → any local‐oscillator drift (even a few Hz) causes distortion.
  • Automatic Frequency Control (AFC): Pilot allows the receiver to lock its local oscillator, maintaining Δf≈0 over long periods.
  • Demodulation Simplicity: Enables a straightforward coherent detector rather than complex VFO stabilization in the receiver.

Mathematical/Power-Efficiency Analysis

1. Pure SSB-SC (J3E) without pilot

All transmitted power (Pt) is in the one sideband:

Ps = Pt, Pc=0, ηSSB = Ps/Pt = 100%.

Challenge: Pc=0 → no reference for demodulation.

2. Pilot-Carrier SSB

Let Ps = sideband power, pilot-carrier power Pc is set to Ps·10-L/10, where L is the attenuation in dB (e.g. 20 dB ⇒ 10-2).

Total power:

Pt = Ps + Pc = Ps(1 + 10-L/10)

Efficiency:

ηpilot = Ps/Pt = 1/(1 + 10-L/10)×100%

Examples:

  • L=16 dB ⇒ Pc/Ps≈0.025 ⇒ η≈97.6%
  • L=26 dB ⇒ Pc/Ps≈0.0025 ⇒ η≈99.75%

3. Conventional AM (DSB with full carrier)

For 100% modulation index m=1:

  • Sideband power =Pc/2 (each sideband share), total sideband =Pc/2.
  • Carrier power =Pc
  • Efficiency:
ηAM = sidebands/(Pc + sidebands) = (Pc/2)/(1.5·Pc)≈33%

Bandwidth Comparison

Scheme Bandwidth
Conventional AM 2B (two sidebands)
Pure SSB (J3E) B
Pilot-Carrier SSB B (plus negligible pilot)

Advantages of Pilot-Carrier SSB vs. Alternatives

1. Vs. Pure SSB-SC

  • + Enables coherent demod/AFC → robust to drift.
  • – Very small power penalty (1–3%).

2. Vs. Conventional AM

  • + Dramatic power efficiency (≈99% vs. ≈33%).
  • + Half the bandwidth (B vs. 2B).
  • + Better range & SNR for the same transmitter power.

3. Practical Receiver Simplification

  • No need for bulky, ultra-stable VFOs or expensive synthesizers.
  • Pilot supports low-cost, tunable receivers with reliable long-term stability.

Summary of Trade-Offs

Feature AM (100% m) SSB-SC (pure) SSB + Pilot
Power Efficiency 33% 100% 97.6–99.75% (depending on L)
Bandwidth 2B B B
Carrier Reference ✓ ✗ ✓ (weak)
Receiver Complexity Low High (drift) Low (AFC-enabled)

Key Takeaway

Pilot-carrier modulation in SSB systems delivers almost all the power- and bandwidth-efficiency benefits of pure SSB while reintroducing just enough carrier (1–3% extra power) to allow simple, stable, coherent demodulation and automatic frequency control—ideal for maritime and long-haul mobile communications.
